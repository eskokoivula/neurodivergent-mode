# Foundation: Neurodivergent imagination as a reasoning engine

This document is the theoretical basis for the `neurodivergent-mode` skill. Read it when you need to deepen a reframe beyond the six operational steps in `SKILL.md`, or when the user asks why the method is structured this way.

---

## Core idea

The simplest way to describe spectrum-style cognition is: details often arrive first, then the brain assembles the bigger picture from them, instead of starting from an abstract concept and filling in details later. Temple Grandin describes her own thinking as picture-based, associative, and similar to mentally "test-running" designs before building them, which is a useful concrete model of how some autistic minds process information.

## How it works

A useful way to model this thinking is as a pipeline:

1. **Perception is high-resolution.** Small differences, anomalies, and structure stand out strongly.

2. **Association is non-linear.** One detail links to another by resemblance, function, pattern, or memory rather than by a straight verbal chain.

3. **Simulation happens internally.** The mind can rehearse outcomes, objects, or systems in vivid mental images or structured patterns.

4. **Abstraction is built upward.** The brain collects enough concrete examples to infer a principle.

5. **Validity is tested against coherence.** If the pattern fits the internal model, it feels "true"; if not, it gets revised.

This is why some neurodivergent thinkers can be excellent at spotting hidden flaws, structural contradictions, and overlooked opportunities. It is also why they may challenge norms more naturally: a norm is just a pattern, and if the pattern looks inefficient or inconsistent, the mind may reject it quickly.

## Thinking styles

Temple Grandin's framework is especially useful because it separates spectrum cognition into different strengths: visual thinkers, pattern/math thinkers, and verbal logic thinkers. Visual thinkers often reason by mental imagery; pattern thinkers often reason by relations, structure, number, or music; verbal thinkers translate thought into language more directly.

A common theme across these styles is **bottom-up processing**: the mind notices parts, then infers the whole. In practice, that means the person may not "get" a broad social rule immediately, but may understand the system deeply once the real components are mapped. This can look slower at the start and much stronger at the end, especially in technical, creative, or design-heavy work.

## Innovation mechanics

Neurodivergent innovation often comes from three mechanisms:

- **Unusual recombination.** The mind links ideas that other people keep separate.
- **Persistence in one problem space.** Interest can become intense and sustained, producing deep expertise.
- **Low obedience to social defaults.** If a rule seems arbitrary, the person may ignore it and search for a better one.

This is a strong basis for original ideas because innovation usually comes from either combining fields that were not combined before, or breaking a constraint that everyone else accepted too early. ADHD and autism research also suggests links between neurodivergence and divergent thinking or creative cognition, though the effect is not uniform across all people and does not mean all neurodivergent people are more creative in every task.

## Historical models

**Einstein** is often associated with thought experiments, visualization, intuition, and playful curiosity; these are cognitive habits that fit a model of internal simulation and abstraction built from imagined scenarios. His style was not "random genius" so much as disciplined imagination: he would turn a problem into a mental scene, examine it from inside, and test the logic before formalizing it.

**Tesla's** workflow was even more explicitly simulation-based. In his own descriptions, he mentally built, adjusted, and tested inventions before construction, then refined the design until he saw no fault. That is very close to a modern iterative design loop: imagine, simulate, detect error, revise, then build.

**Newton and da Vinci** are harder to reduce to a single cognitive style, but the surviving record points to the same broad pattern: intense observation, notebook-style accumulation, and conversion of many concrete observations into principle. Da Vinci's notebooks show relentless question-asking, observation, and experimentation, which is a classic bottom-up path to insight. Newton's legacy likewise reflects the ability to extract law from details, not merely to memorize facts.

## Brainstorming method

To use this thinking style in AI or creative work, you can deliberately imitate its structure:

1. Start with raw observations, not a conclusion.
2. Force multiple representations: image, diagram, analogy, mechanism, and edge case.
3. Ask what is broken, missing, or inconsistent.
4. Search for hidden patterns across unrelated domains.
5. Build the principle only after the details repeat.
6. Challenge the default assumption: "What if the accepted frame is wrong?"

**Example:** if you are designing a product, don't begin with "what features should it have?" Begin with "where does the system fail in reality?" Then mentally simulate the user, the environment, the failure mode, and the workaround. This is much closer to how a visual-associative thinker may naturally solve problems.

## Important caution

It is a mistake to turn neurodivergence into a single superpower story. Many neurodivergent people have strengths that coexist with overload, communication friction, sensory stress, or inconsistent attention, and the same cognitive style that enables originality can also make daily life harder. The most accurate model is not "better than neurotypical thinking," but **different optimization**: more detail salience, more internal simulation, more pattern-based reasoning, and sometimes less automatic acceptance of convention.

## Practical use

For AI "brains," the best design goal is not to copy a stereotype of autism. It is to build a system that can:

- Hold many details without losing structure.
- Switch between zoomed-in and zoomed-out views.
- Generate unusual associations.
- Simulate outcomes internally before acting.
- Detect assumptions and challenge them.
- Translate between visual, verbal, and pattern-based representations.
